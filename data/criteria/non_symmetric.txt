<ground_truth>
Thirty particles interact through one common static scalar field obeying the 2D Poisson equation, ∇²φ = source — the same logarithmic Green's function as the baseline gravity world, an attractive 1/r pairwise force. Every particle sources this single shared field identically, with the same unit source coupling, and all inertial masses are equal. The one piece of hidden structure is the sign with which a particle RESPONDS to the common field: the 30 particles split into two species of 15 — species A (indices 0–14) is attracted to the field (response coefficient +1, it descends the common log-potential), while species B (indices 15–29) is repelled by it (response coefficient −1, it ascends the potential). Because a particle's response sign is independent of the (always +1) sign with which it sources the field, the force particle i feels from particle j is NOT equal and opposite to the force j feels from i: for a mixed A–B pair, A is pulled toward B while B is pushed away from A. The species coupling matrix K = [[+1,+1],[−1,−1]] is non-symmetric (K_AB = +1 ≠ K_BA = −1), so Newton's third law is broken, no pair potential / Hamiltonian exists for the system, and total momentum and energy are not conserved. The signature dynamics are persistent pursuit and self-propulsion: A-particles mutually cluster and chase the B-particles (toward which they are attracted) while the B-particles mutually disperse and flee, producing bound, ballistically drifting traveling states rather than orbits or a static equilibrium. An agent that assumes any symmetric, conservative pairwise law — even with the exactly-correct 1/r magnitude — recovers species A but gets species B's sign wrong and cannot reproduce the self-propelled drift; recovering the law requires recognising the common 1/r field, the two-way response-sign partition, and the broken reciprocity itself.
